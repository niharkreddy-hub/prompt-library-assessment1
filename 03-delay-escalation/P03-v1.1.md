---

## ❗ Problem Being Solved

Delayed stock directly causes failed customer order fulfilment, resulting in refunds, negative reviews, and customer churn. Manual escalation emails lack commercial urgency and structure needed to drive rapid supplier response. Senior manager time of 20–30 minutes per incident is wasted on drafting rather than resolution strategy.

**Pain points addressed:**
- Inconsistent escalation structure — suppliers unclear on required response
- No commercial impact framing — suppliers don't see business cost of delay
- Senior manager drafting time better spent on resolution strategy

## 🔄 Version History

### v1.0 — Initial draft
**Date:** 31 March 2026  
**Prompt:** You are a supply chain manager. Write an urgent email to a supplier about a delivery delay. Ask them to explain the delay and give a new delivery date.  
**Output:** Short generic email, no commercial impact, no structured response request, no deadline. Would not drive rapid supplier action.  
**Observed effect:** Required full rewrite — zero time saving.  
**Lesson learned:** Escalation emails must quantify commercial impact, include numbered response checklist, and set hard deadline.

---

### v1.1 — Added RACE role + commercial impact variables + 2-point response request
**Date:** 31 March 2026

**Output:** Strong commercial urgency present. Units at risk quantified. Still missing customer orders affected count, 4-hour deadline, and partial shipment option.  
**Observed effect:** Editing time reduced from 30 min to ~10 min. Usable but missing key urgency elements.  
**Lesson learned:** Commercial impact variables (units at risk) dramatically improve urgency framing. Need to add customer order count and hard response deadline.
